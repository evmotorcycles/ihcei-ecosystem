Abstract

We propose the Telemetric Metric, in which proper distance is the round-trip information latency between two regions of a correlation network, and the theory Latency–Metric Duality, in which geometry is emergent bookkeeping of that latency rather than a fundamental container. We report a pre-registered numerical validation: the specification — parameters, seeds, and pass thresholds — was locked under a canonical SHA-256 fingerprint before the run. Across 40 random correlation networks the metric is genuine (0/8640 triangle violations), obeys the predicted scaling d ∝ 1/√coupling (log-log slope −0.5000, R²=1.00000), and the primary discriminator — hold two sites at fixed positions, sweep only their coupling — shows the emergent distance moving (range 0.94) while a fundamental-container null stays frozen (0). We then specify the physical experiment that would decide the ontological question, and its falsifiable signature. The physical experiment is proposed, not performed; the claim that physical spacetime is emergent is neither claimed nor proven.

1 · Physics advances by turning latency into law

Einstein's E=mc² began as a telemetry problem — synchronizing clocks across distance with light signals, where the finite round-trip time of a sync pulse encodes the relation between two clocks. He read that delay as a fixed geometry. This paper runs the move in reverse: what if the geometry was never fixed — what if distance is just the latency, all the way down? 2 · The equation & the theory

For a network given only by a symmetric coupling matrix W, the effective resistance is R_ij = L⁺_ii + L⁺_jj − 2L⁺_ij (with L the graph Laplacian), the commute time is C_ij = vol·R_ij, and the round-trip latency is τ_rt = C_ij/ν = R_ij. Two regions are close when a signal round-trips fast, far when it is slow. Latency–Metric Duality: where general relativity says matter tells space how to curve, LMD says correlation tells latency how to render as distance — the entanglement-geometry family (Van Raamsdonk, Ryu–Takayanagi, ER=EPR), stated as a benchable latency law.
